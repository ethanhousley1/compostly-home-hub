Composting is nature's recycling. It's the process of breaking down organic material — food scraps, yard trimmings, paper — into a rich, soil-like substance called **compost**. That finished material gets returned to the earth to improve soil health, grow healthier plants, and close the loop on organic waste.

Things that were once alive decompose, and the nutrients they held get recycled back into the soil where new life can use them.

## How it works

Billions of microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, decomposers — consume organic material and break it down into simpler compounds. Four things keep them working:

- **Browns** (carbon) — dried leaves, cardboard, straw. Energy for microbes.
- **Greens** (nitrogen) — food scraps, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings. Fuel for microbial growth.
- **Water** — decomposers need moisture. A healthy pile feels like a wrung-out sponge.
- **Air** — most composting microbes are aerobic. Turning the pile keeps oxygen flowing.

When these are in balance, your pile heats up and produces finished compost in as little as 4–8 weeks.

## Why it matters

**Organic waste in landfills doesn't compost — it ferments.** Roughly 24% of what Americans throw away is food and organic material. Buried without oxygen, it produces methane, a greenhouse gas 28× more potent than CO₂.

Composting the same material aerobically returns carbon to the soil instead of the atmosphere, builds soil structure and microbial life, and reduces the need for synthetic fertilizers.
