A quick-reference guide to the most commonly used terms in home composting.

---

**Aerobic decomposition**
Decomposition that occurs in the presence of oxygen. Aerobic composting is faster, produces more heat, and generates very little odor compared to anaerobic decomposition. The goal of turning your pile regularly is to maintain aerobic conditions.

---

**Anaerobic decomposition**
Decomposition that occurs without oxygen. Anaerobic conditions develop when a pile is too wet, too compacted, or not turned. The result is a slow, cold, smelly process that produces methane — the same thing that happens to organic waste buried in a landfill.

---

**Bioavailability**
How readily nutrients in compost can be taken up by plant roots. Finished, stable compost releases nutrients slowly and steadily (good for plants). Immature or "hot" compost releases nutrients too rapidly and can burn roots.

---

**Browns**
Carbon-rich materials added to a compost pile. Dried leaves, cardboard, straw, paper, and wood chips are common browns. They provide energy for microorganisms and absorb excess moisture. See also: [C:N ratio](#cn-ratio).

---

**C:N ratio (Carbon-to-Nitrogen ratio)**
The ratio of carbon to nitrogen in your compost pile. The ideal range for active decomposition is roughly 25–30:1. In practice, this means adding about 3 parts browns to 1 part greens by volume. A pile that is too high in carbon (all browns) decomposes very slowly. Too much nitrogen (all greens) produces ammonia odors.

---

**Curing**
A resting period of 2–4 weeks after the main decomposition phase is complete. Curing stabilizes the microbial activity and produces a gentler, more uniform product safe to use directly on plant roots and seedlings. Compostly compost is always fully cured before distribution.

---

**Finished compost (humus)**
Fully decomposed organic matter that has reached a stable state. It is dark brown to black, crumbly, earthy-smelling, and no longer recognizable as its original source materials. Also called "humus" (not to be confused with the dip).

---

**Greens**
Nitrogen-rich materials added to a compost pile. Fresh food scraps, coffee grounds, grass clippings, and plant trimmings are common greens. They provide protein and moisture that fuel microbial growth.

---

**Hot composting**
An active composting method that maintains pile temperatures between 120–160°F through careful C:N balance, moisture management, and regular turning. Hot composting can produce finished compost in as little as 4–8 weeks and kills most weed seeds and pathogens.

---

**Humus**
See *finished compost*. The stable, carbon-rich end product of decomposition. Humus improves soil structure, water retention, and microbial diversity.

---

**Leachate**
The dark liquid that sometimes drains from the bottom of a compost bin or worm bin. It is concentrated with nutrients and soluble compounds. Dilute 10:1 with water before applying to plants as a liquid fertilizer, or add it back into the pile.

---

**Mesophilic bacteria**
Microorganisms that thrive at moderate temperatures (50–115°F). They dominate the early and late stages of decomposition, before and after the high-heat thermophilic phase.

---

**Thermophilic bacteria**
Heat-loving microorganisms that dominate the hot core of an actively decomposing pile (115–160°F). Thermophilic activity is what kills weed seeds and pathogens in hot composting.

---

**Turning**
The act of physically mixing and aerating a compost pile to introduce oxygen, redistribute moisture, and move cooler outer material into the hotter center. More frequent turning = faster decomposition.

---

**Vermicompost (worm castings)**
Compost produced by worms, specifically red wigglers (*Eisenia fetida*). Worm castings are exceptionally nutrient-dense, biologically active, and gentle enough to use directly on seedlings. The process of producing vermicompost is called vermicomposting.

---

**Worm bin**
A container housing composting worms (typically red wigglers) that process kitchen scraps into vermicompost. Worm bins work well indoors, making them ideal for apartments and small spaces. See [Getting Started at Home](/docs/guides/getting-started-at-home/#worm-bin-vermicomposting) for setup instructions.

---

**Windrow**
A long, narrow pile of composting material used in large-scale or commercial composting operations. Not typically relevant for home composting, but the same aerobic principles apply — the windrow is turned mechanically for aeration.
