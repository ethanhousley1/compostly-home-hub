Not everything organic composts equally well. Understanding the difference between **greens** and **browns** — and keeping a rough balance between them — is the single most important thing you can do to keep your pile healthy and odor-free.

## Greens vs. browns

Composting is driven by microorganisms, and like any living things, they need both **energy** (from carbon) and **protein** (from nitrogen) to thrive.

| | Greens | Browns |
|---|---|---|
| **What they are** | Nitrogen-rich, moist materials | Carbon-rich, dry materials |
| **Role in the pile** | Fuel microbial growth | Provide energy, add structure, absorb moisture |
| **Examples** | Fruit & veggie scraps, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings | Dried leaves, cardboard, straw, paper |
| **Smell when too much** | Slimy, ammonia odor | Dry pile, very slow to break down |

### The carbon-to-nitrogen (C:N) ratio

The ideal C:N ratio for a hot, fast compost pile is roughly **25–30 parts carbon to 1 part nitrogen** by weight. You don't need to measure this precisely — a simple rule of thumb is:

> **Aim for roughly 3 parts browns to 1 part greens by volume.**

In practice, most households generate far more greens (kitchen scraps) than browns. Keep a bag of dried leaves, a roll of cardboard, or a bag of wood chips nearby to layer in whenever you add food scraps.

## Quick reference: yes and no

### Yes — add freely

| Material | Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Fruit & vegetable scraps | Green | All types, including citrus peels and onions |
| Coffee grounds | Green | Include paper filters |
| Tea leaves | Green | Remove staples from paper bags |
| Eggshells | Neutral | Crush them to speed breakdown |
| Fresh grass clippings | Green | Mix with browns to avoid matting |
| Dried leaves | Brown | Shred for faster decomposition |
| Cardboard (plain) | Brown | Tear into small pieces; remove tape |
| Newspaper & office paper | Brown | Shred first |
| Paper bags, napkins, towels | Brown | Unbleached preferred |
| Straw | Brown | Great bulking agent |
| Hair and nail clippings | Green | Decompose slowly but fine |
| Houseplant trimmings | Green | Avoid diseased plants |
| Wood chips (untreated) | Brown | Excellent for drainage and structure |

### Avoid in a home bin

| Material | Why to avoid |
|---|---|
| Meat and fish scraps | Attract pests; produce strong odors |
| Dairy products | Same as above |
| Oily or greasy foods | Repels water, slows decomposition, attracts pests |
| Diseased plants | Pathogens can survive and spread to your garden |
| Pet waste (dogs, cats) | Contains harmful pathogens |
| Treated or painted wood | Leaches toxins |
| Coal ash | Contains sulfur and iron compounds harmful to plants |
| Weed seed heads | Seeds may survive and sprout in your garden |

## Balancing your pile in practice

Here's a simple workflow for the kitchen:

1. Collect food scraps in a countertop bin throughout the day.
2. When you add scraps to your outdoor pile, layer in a roughly equal volume of browns (dried leaves work great).
3. If your pile smells like ammonia or feels slimy — add more browns.
4. If your pile feels bone dry and isn't breaking down — add more greens, or a splash of water.

That's the whole formula. You'll develop an instinct for it quickly.

## What about "compostable" packaging?

Certified compostable packaging (marked with the BPI or TUV logo) is **not suitable for most home bins**. These plastics require sustained temperatures above 140°F to break down properly — temperatures that only industrial facilities reliably reach. If you try to compost them at home, they'll sit in your pile for months or years without degrading.

Drop certified compostable packaging in your Compostly bucket instead, and we'll route it to an industrial facility.
