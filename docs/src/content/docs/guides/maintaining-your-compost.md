A compost pile is a living system. Once it's up and running, your main job is to keep conditions right for the billions of microorganisms doing the actual work. That mostly means three things: **turning, moisture, and balance.**

## Turning your pile

Turning introduces oxygen. Most composting microbes are aerobic — they need air — and decomposition slows dramatically when a pile compacts and oxygen runs out. Turning fluffs the pile, moves cooler outer material into the hotter center, and jump-starts decomposition.

### How often to turn

| Goal | Turning frequency |
|---|---|
| Fast, hot compost (4–8 weeks) | Every 3–5 days |
| Active but relaxed management | Once a week |
| Cold/slow composting | Every few weeks or when you think of it |

For a **tumbler**, spin it 5–10 full rotations every 2–3 days.

For a **worm bin**, you don't turn in the traditional sense — instead, gently fluff the bedding surface with a fork once a week to prevent compaction.

### Signs your pile needs turning

- The center feels hot and dense with no airflow
- You notice an ammonia smell (pile is too anaerobic)
- Decomposition has visibly slowed

### Tools

A long-handled pitchfork or a dedicated compost aerator tool works well. Aim to move material from the outside edges into the center where it's hottest.

## Moisture management

Think of your pile like a **wrung-out sponge** — it should be damp throughout but not dripping. Moisture is essential because decomposer microbes live in the thin water film around particles. Too dry, and they go dormant. Too wet, and the pile goes anaerobic and starts to smell.

### Checking moisture

Grab a handful of material from the middle of the pile and squeeze. You should see a few drops of water. If nothing comes out, it's too dry. If water streams freely, it's too wet.

### Adjusting

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Pile is too dry | Water it in layers as you turn; add wet greens |
| Pile is too wet | Add dry browns (shredded leaves, cardboard); turn to increase airflow |
| Surface dries out fast | Cover with a tarp or burlap during dry weather |

In rainy climates, a loose cover or lid helps prevent waterlogging. In dry climates, you may need to water your pile like a garden bed every week or two.

## Troubleshooting common problems

### Bad smell

Compost should smell earthy — like soil or a forest floor. Off-odors are a diagnostic signal:

| Smell | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Ammonia / sharp chemical | Too much nitrogen (greens); anaerobic conditions | Add browns; turn to aerate |
| Rotten eggs / sulfur | Waterlogged, anaerobic | Drain excess moisture; add coarse browns; turn aggressively |
| Sweet / fermenting | Fruit scraps fermenting before microbes catch up | Mix in more browns; bury fruit scraps deeper |
| No smell at all, very slow breakdown | Too dry, or not enough nitrogen | Check moisture; add greens |

### Pests

A well-managed compost bin isn't particularly attractive to pests. Most problems stem from adding the wrong materials or leaving food scraps exposed.

**Prevention:**
- Always bury food scraps under a layer of browns
- Don't add meat, fish, dairy, or oily foods to a home bin
- Use a bin with a secure lid or a rodent-resistant base (hardware cloth under an open pile works well)
- Keep the pile away from fences and structures where rodents nest

**If you already have pests:**
1. Turn the pile to expose and disrupt any tunneling
2. Remove any attractants (meat, cooked food) and bury or transfer to your Compostly bucket
3. Line the base of your bin with hardware cloth (1/4-inch mesh) to block entry from below
4. Add a layer of soil or finished compost on top to mask odors

### Slow decomposition

If your pile hasn't changed much in several weeks:

1. **Check the C:N ratio** — if it's mostly dry browns, add greens. If it's mostly wet scraps, add browns.
2. **Check moisture** — a dry pile barely decomposes. Water it and turn.
3. **Check pile size** — piles smaller than 3×3×3 feet struggle to retain heat. Combine multiple small piles.
4. **Shred materials** — smaller pieces have more surface area for microbes. Tear cardboard, shred leaves, chop large scraps.
5. **Add a nitrogen boost** — a shovelful of fresh grass clippings, coffee grounds, or a small amount of blood meal can re-ignite a slow pile.

### Pile won't heat up

A hot pile (120–160°F in the center) means microbial activity is high and decomposition is fast. If your pile stays cool:

- It may be too small — heat requires enough mass to be self-insulating
- It may be finished — if it looks uniform and earthy, it might already be done
- It may be too dry — check moisture first
- It may need nitrogen — add more greens

A cool pile will still compost eventually, just more slowly. That's fine if you're not in a rush.

## Seasonal adjustments

**Summer:** Piles can dry out quickly. Check moisture weekly and water as needed. Shading the pile slightly can help in extreme heat.

**Fall:** An abundance of leaves is a gift — bag and store extras to use as browns throughout the year.

**Winter:** Decomposition slows significantly below 50°F and stops near freezing. Keep adding material through winter; the pile will "wake up" and decompose rapidly when spring arrives. Insulating the outside of the bin with straw bales helps in cold climates.

**Spring:** Turn your wintered pile to re-introduce oxygen and get things moving again.
