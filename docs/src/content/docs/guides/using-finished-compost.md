All the work of building and maintaining your pile leads to this: finished compost — a dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling material that's one of the most valuable things you can add to your soil. Here's how to know when it's ready and how to get the most out of it.

## How to tell when compost is finished

Finished compost has a few unmistakable characteristics:

- **Dark brown to black color** — uniform throughout, no visible red or bright material
- **Earthy smell** — like a forest floor after rain, not sharp or sour
- **Crumbly texture** — breaks apart easily; no recognizable food scraps or plant material
- **Reduced volume** — a finished pile is typically 50–70% smaller than when you started
- **Cool temperature** — a previously hot pile that has cooled and stabilized is a strong indicator

If you can still identify individual scraps (orange peels, eggshells, leaves), give it more time. Eggshells in particular take a long time to fully break down — their presence doesn't mean the rest isn't ready, but most other material should be unrecognizable.

### The bag test

If you're unsure, scoop some finished-looking material into a sealed plastic bag and leave it in a warm spot for a week. If it smells foul when you open it, it's still actively decomposing and not ready. If it still smells earthy, it's finished.

---

## Curing (optional but recommended)

Even when the main decomposition is complete, **curing** — letting the compost rest for 2–4 additional weeks in a separate pile — stabilizes the microbial activity and produces a gentler, more uniform product. This step is especially important if you plan to use compost in direct contact with plant roots or seedlings.

To cure:
1. Move finished-looking compost to a separate bin or pile
2. Keep it slightly moist and loosely covered
3. Leave it undisturbed for 2–4 weeks
4. It's ready when it remains stable and earthy-smelling

Compostly's facility-processed compost is always fully cured before distribution.

---

## Sifting your compost

Home piles often contain a mix of finished and partially finished material. **Sifting** separates the two:

1. Build or buy a simple sifter (a wooden frame with 1/2-inch hardware cloth works well)
2. Pass compost through the screen — finished material falls through, chunks stay on top
3. Return the unfinished chunks to your active pile
4. Use the fine, sifted compost wherever precision matters (seed starting, potting mixes)

Sifting isn't required for most garden applications, but it produces a more consistent product.

## How to use finished compost

### Garden beds and vegetable gardens

**How to apply:** Spread a 1–3 inch layer over your beds and work it into the top 4–6 inches of soil with a fork or tiller.

**When:** In spring before planting, or in fall after harvest to let it continue breaking down over winter.

**Rate:** 1–3 inches per season is plenty. More is not always better — excessive compost in a small bed can lead to nutrient imbalances.

---

### Lawn top-dressing

**How to apply:** Spread a thin layer (1/4 to 1/2 inch) across your lawn after aerating or mowing. Work it into the thatch with a rake or drag mat.

**When:** Spring or fall are ideal — grass is actively growing and will incorporate the compost quickly.

**Benefits:** Improves soil structure, supports lawn microbiome, reduces thatch buildup, and often reduces the need for synthetic fertilizers.

---

### Potting mixes

Finished, cured, sifted compost can be blended into potting mix at a ratio of **20–30% compost to 70–80% potting base** (a mix of perlite and coco coir or peat works well). Don't use more than 30% compost in a container mix — it can retain too much water and compact over time.

:::caution[Use only fully finished compost in containers]
Partially decomposed compost in a closed container continues to break down and can heat up, create anaerobic pockets, and damage plant roots. Cured, sifted compost only.
:::

---

### Around trees and shrubs

Apply a 2–3 inch layer of compost as mulch around the base of trees and shrubs, keeping it a few inches away from the trunk. This suppresses weeds, retains moisture, and slowly feeds the soil as it breaks down further.

---

### Seed starting

Mix fully finished, sifted compost at 10–20% into your seed-starting medium. Too much can inhibit germination by retaining excess moisture or providing nutrient levels too high for delicate seedlings.

---

## Storing leftover compost

Finished compost stores well for 1–2 years if kept:

- **Covered** — a tarp, lid, or covered bin prevents nutrient leaching from rain and keeps it from drying out
- **Slightly moist** — bone-dry compost loses beneficial microbial activity
- **In a breathable container** — avoid airtight storage, which can create anaerobic conditions

Clumping or white mold on stored compost is generally harmless and indicates a normal microbial process. Break it up and mix it before use.
