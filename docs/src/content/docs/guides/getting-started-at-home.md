Starting a home compost system doesn't require much space, money, or time. The hardest part is deciding which method fits your life — after that, the microbes do most of the work.

## Step 1: Choose your composting method

### Open pile or bin

The simplest approach. You layer organic material in an outdoor bin (or just a pile in the corner of your yard) and let it break down over time.

**Best for:** Households with outdoor space and a steady supply of yard waste (leaves, grass clippings).

**Pros:**
- Lowest cost — a basic bin can be built from pallets or wire fencing
- Handles large volumes of material
- Low maintenance if you're patient

**Cons:**
- Slower decomposition (2–12 months without active management)
- Requires some outdoor space
- More exposed to pests if not managed well

**Good starter bins:** A simple wooden pallet enclosure, a wire mesh cylinder (about 3 ft in diameter), or a commercial plastic compost bin with a lid.

---

### Compost tumbler

A sealed rotating drum mounted on a frame. You load it with organic material, give it a spin every few days to aerate, and it produces finished compost in 4–8 weeks.

**Best for:** Households that want faster results and better pest control than an open pile.

**Pros:**
- Faster decomposition when spun regularly
- Enclosed design keeps pests out
- Neater appearance

**Cons:**
- Higher upfront cost ($60–$150)
- Limited volume
- Can dry out quickly — check moisture regularly

---

### Worm bin (vermicomposting)

Redworms (specifically *Eisenia fetida*, or "red wigglers") break down kitchen scraps in a compact, indoor-friendly bin. The output — worm castings — is one of the most nutrient-dense soil amendments available.

**Best for:** Apartment dwellers, small households, or anyone who primarily generates kitchen scraps with little yard waste.

**Pros:**
- Works indoors (under the sink, in a closet, on a balcony)
- Very fast processing — 2–4 months to finished castings
- Worm castings are exceptional compost quality
- A great activity for kids

**Cons:**
- Requires purchasing worms (~$30–$50 for a starter pound)
- More sensitive to temperature extremes (keep between 55–77°F)
- Doesn't handle large quantities of material as easily as outdoor bins
- Worms need a few weeks to get established

## Step 2: Pick your location

For **outdoor piles and tumblers:**

- **Partial shade** — full sun dries the pile out too fast; full shade slows it down
- **Well-drained ground** — avoid low spots that collect standing water
- **Convenient but not prominent** — close enough to your kitchen that you'll actually use it, but out of sight if aesthetics matter
- **Away from fences and wooden structures** — decomposing material can trap moisture against wood over time
- **At least 10 feet from a well** — to protect groundwater

For **worm bins:**

- **Temperature-stable spot** — a garage, basement, or shaded porch works well in most climates
- **Away from direct sunlight** — worms are sensitive to heat and will try to escape if the bin gets too warm

## Step 3: Build your first pile

### For an open bin or pile

1. **Start with a brown base.** Lay 3–4 inches of coarse browns (wood chips, straw, or torn cardboard) on the ground. This creates airflow at the bottom.
2. **Add a green layer.** Add 1–2 inches of nitrogen-rich material — food scraps, fresh grass clippings, coffee grounds.
3. **Cover with browns.** Add another 2–3 inch layer of dry leaves or shredded paper on top. This controls odors and balances the C:N ratio.
4. **Moisten lightly.** If materials are dry, add water until the pile feels like a wrung-out sponge — damp but not dripping.
5. **Repeat the layers** as you add new material. Always finish with a brown layer on top.

Your pile needs to be at least **3 feet × 3 feet × 3 feet** to generate and retain enough heat for active decomposition. Smaller piles will still break down — just more slowly.

### For a tumbler

Fill the drum about halfway before you start spinning — a tumbler that's too empty or too full won't turn efficiently. Follow the same greens/browns layering principle, and spin it every 2–3 days to aerate.

### For a worm bin

1. Prepare bedding: shredded newspaper or cardboard, dampened to 70–80% moisture (squeeze a handful — it should hold its shape but not drip).
2. Add your worms and let them settle in for 1–2 days before adding food.
3. Bury food scraps in the bedding rather than leaving them on top — this reduces fruit flies.
4. Start small: a pound of worms can process about half a pound of food per day.

## What to expect in the first few weeks

- **Days 1–7:** Microbial activity picks up, the pile may warm noticeably. You might see steam on cold mornings.
- **Weeks 2–4:** Volume shrinks as material breaks down. A hot pile might reach 130–160°F in the center.
- **Months 1–3:** With regular turning, the pile cools and begins to look more soil-like. You'll stop recognizing individual scraps.
- **Months 3–6+:** Without turning, cold composting is slower but still effective. Be patient.
